The reply arrives on Sunday evening, while she is drying a plate she has already dried once.

She almost misses it — the researcher's account doesn't trigger any notification she's set up, because of course it doesn't, this was never meant to go through a system that notifies her of things. She checks the inbox out of habit, the same habit that has checked it and found nothing eleven times this past week, and this time there is something.

The subject line is careful. *Re: Sector 9, Interval Study.*

She sets the plate down.

*Thank you for sending this. I want to be honest with you about a few things before we go further, because I think you deserve that.*

*I get documents like this more often than you'd expect — not this good, and not usually unsolicited from inside the system itself, but the general shape isn't rare. Most of them go nowhere. I want you to understand that going in, so that whatever comes next isn't a bigger disappointment than it needs to be.*

*But I read all twenty-two pages twice. The interval framing is not something I've seen articulated this precisely before — most critiques of the allocation model attack the inputs or the outputs. You're describing something in between, in the gap the model was never built to see, and you're describing it with names and dates and a level of specificity that changes what kind of document this is.*

*I would like to speak with you. I understand if that's not possible, or not yet. If it helps: I don't need your name for a first conversation. I need to understand what you're willing to risk, and what you're not, before I ask you to risk any more of it.*

*I am sorry about Sector 9. I mean that plainly, not as a rhetorical gesture. I have read enough of these to know what "sorry" is worth against a process already in motion. Not much. I'm saying it anyway.*

She reads it three times, standing at the counter with the tea towel still in her hand.

Celia comes in, sees her face, and doesn't ask.

Mara goes to her room and sits on the edge of the bed with her notebook open on her knees, not writing anything yet, just holding the pencil the way you hold something before you've decided what to do with it.

She thinks: *someone else knows now. Someone who isn't Elias, who isn't inside any of this.*

She thinks: *that changes the shape of the thing. It's not just ours anymore.*

She writes, eventually, one line: *She read it. She wants to talk.*

She does not go to Sector 9 that night. It's late, and it can wait until she has words worth carrying there.
