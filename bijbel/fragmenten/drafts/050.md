Wednesday brings a cross-division access audit, which is not her usual work but which lands on Verification when Assessment is short-staffed, and she takes it because a full day of something else is, right now, a relief.

The audit is simple in structure and tedious in volume: confirm that clearance levels match role designations across a sample of Sector 2 accounts, flag discrepancies, close what matches. Sector 2 is not a place she thinks about often. High relevance, full Accord coverage, the kind of district that generates almost no anomalies because almost nothing there is allowed to become one.

She works through the list. Housing coordinator, clearance matches. Medical intake supervisor, clearance matches. Systems integration architect, Lena Voss, clearance matches — access level consistent with role, last review eighteen months ago, no flags.

She moves to the next line.

There are two hundred and six names on the list. She will not remember most of them by the time she leaves tonight. She does not, in this moment, remember why this one snags — only that it does, briefly, the way a word can feel foreign the fortieth time you say it in a row, the meaning briefly detaching from the sound. She glances back at the entry. *Voss.* An ordinary name. She has processed thousands of ordinary names.

She closes the item and moves on.

At 15:40 she takes a break she doesn't usually take, stands at the window with a cup of water she isn't thirsty for, and watches the tram line curve away toward the older sectors, toward the direction she doesn't take on her way home anymore without a reason.

She doesn't think about the name again that afternoon. It sits somewhere below the level she checks, filed the way everything gets filed here — correctly, efficiently, and without the system, or her, understanding yet what it will turn out to matter to.

She finishes the audit by 17:00. One hundred and ninety-four clearances confirmed. Twelve flagged for review, none of them Sector 2, none of them anyone she'll think about again.

She goes home. She does not write about her day in the notebook. There is nothing, as far as she can tell, worth writing down.

Somewhere in the archive, unremarked, a record now shows that Verification reviewed Lena Voss's file on May 1st and found nothing wrong with it at all.
