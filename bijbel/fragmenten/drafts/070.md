She thinks about it on the tram the next morning, and keeps thinking about it through her queue, through Petra's small talk, through the whole ordinary architecture of a Wednesday at Meridian Street.

*Someone I lost. To silence. My own silence, mostly.*

She has spent two months believing she understood what the garden was — a secret, a refuge, the thing a man builds when the world stops having room for him. She understands now, turning last night's sentence over, that she had it backward, or at least incomplete. The garden isn't where he hides from what he did. It's the one place he's still telling the truth.

Everywhere else — the retirement, the silence, the years of not-speaking — was the hiding. The garden, improbably, is the confession. Every row he plants is an admission that he still believes in something he has no right, by his own accounting, to believe in: that care matters, that attention matters, that you can be responsible for a harm and still be worth something, if what you do with what's left of yourself is honest.

She understands, too, why he told her what he did last night and not more. It isn't secrecy. It's pacing — the careful, deliberate pacing of a man handing over pieces of himself in an order he can survive, rather than all at once in a way that would flatten him.

She doesn't press him for what comes next. She has learned that much, at least, about loving someone who has spent this long protecting a wound: that the asking has to come from inside him, on his schedule, or it isn't really given at all, just extracted.

At lunch, she writes in her notebook, briefly, in the stairwell where nobody can see her: *He said "someone I lost." Not "something." Someone.*

She underlines the word once, and closes the notebook, and does not let herself guess further, though the guess is already there, at the edge of her, the way a shape is there in fog before you can name what it is.

She goes back to her queue. Thirty-nine items. She closes them steadily, and finds, for the first time in weeks, that the work itself — not the meaning underneath it, just the plain mechanical act of checking one thing against another — is almost restful.
