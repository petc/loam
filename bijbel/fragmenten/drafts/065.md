The formal notice arrives in her queue on Friday morning, and unlike the sector-wide notification months ago, this one has her name on it.

*NOTICE OF RECORD — UNAUTHORIZED ACCESS TO RESTRICTED MATERIALS.* Not a dismissal. Not even, technically, a reprimand — the language is careful in a way she now recognises as deliberate, the same passive architecture Elias described in the ZVA documentation, built to be true and useless and slightly threatening all at the same time. *A review of your account activity has identified access patterns inconsistent with your role scope. This notice is recorded to your file. No further action is required at this time, pending continued monitoring.*

Pending continued monitoring.

She reads it four times at her desk before she lets herself react, because reacting where anyone can see her is not something she can afford right now.

Her supervisor doesn't call her in this time. That, she understands, is the point — the notice does the work a conversation used to do, colder and better documented, no witness needed, no chance for her to explain herself into a sympathetic reading. It simply exists now, attached to her file, a permanent fact about her that will be visible to anyone who ever runs a background check on her again, for as long as she works inside any system connected to this one.

She thinks: *this is what the model does to Sector 9, at a smaller scale, to me.* A single flag. Nothing dramatic. Just a fact recorded, quietly compounding whatever it touches next.

She goes to the stairwell where the signal is bad and reads the notice one more time on her phone, as if a fifth reading might change what it says.

It doesn't.

She thinks about the researcher's question — *are you safe* — and understands, finally, in a way she couldn't when it was first asked, that the honest answer was always going to arrive exactly like this: not as danger, but as erosion. A record. A pending. A door that used to be open, now recorded as slightly ajar.

She does not go to Sector 9 that evening either. She sits with it first. She has learned that some things need to be carried alone for a while before they can be set down in front of someone who loves you, so that what you hand them is the thing itself and not just your fear of it.

She writes only one line in the notebook that night: *It has my name on it now.*
