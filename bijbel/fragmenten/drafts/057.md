She is looking for something else entirely when she finds it.

It's a slow Wednesday, her queue thinner than usual, and she's doing what she does now on slow days — not searching Sector 9 directly, she's learned that lesson, but running quiet, defensible little queries around its edges, things a bored verifier might plausibly do. Staffing allocation history. Divisional routing logs. The kind of metadata nobody reviews unless they're already looking for a reason to.

She isn't looking for herself. That's the part that catches her off guard.

Her own name surfaces in a routing log by accident, a side effect of a query she built to trace how Sector 9 cases get assigned across Verification's rotation. She almost skips past it. Then she reads the timestamp again.

Her assignment to the Sector 9 rotation — the one that put the original heat-signature flag in her queue on March 15th, the one that started all of this — was not, as she'd always assumed, the ordinary output of the rotation algorithm.

It was a manual override. Logged four days before the flag reached her. Authorising division: redacted, the same kind of redaction she'd found on the Northern Expansion authorisation, the pattern she now recognises the shape of even blacked out.

She sits very still.

She runs it twice more, from different angles, hoping for a different answer both times. She gets the same one.

Someone routed that flag to her, specifically, four days before it existed in any queue she could see. Not the algorithm. A person, or a process standing in for a person, deciding that this particular anomaly, in this particular sector, should land on this particular desk.

Her desk.

She thinks about every choice she's made since — walking to the fence, going back, going back again, closing the flag the way she did, the specific quality of curiosity she'd always experienced as entirely her own, private, unprompted.

She thinks: *was any of it mine?*

She closes the log. She does not write this in her notebook tonight. She isn't ready to make it that permanent, not yet, not until she understands what it means, if it means anything at all beyond an administrative accident dressed up, by her own frightened imagination, as something larger.

She sits at her desk long after her shift ends, the ceiling panels beginning their slow fade, and does not move to leave.
