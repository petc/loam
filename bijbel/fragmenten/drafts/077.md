The Whitlocks' denial arrives on Wednesday, and she knows before she opens the file, because the reference code alone tells her which template it uses.

*Appeal denied. Grounds: insufficient demonstrated deviation from model-projected outcomes. No further appeal available at this determination level.*

She reads it standing at her terminal, the queue around it entirely unrelated, ordinary, indifferent — a census reconciliation two lines above, a maintenance flag two lines below, the machinery of the Accord continuing to process everything with exactly the same tone regardless of what any individual item costs the person on the other end of it.

She thinks about Mrs. Whitlock's folder, the tenancy records going back to 2029, the careful sentences Elias helped them build about six years running the water point rotation, about teaching children who had nowhere else to learn to read. None of it, in the end, counted as deviation from what the model already expected. The model expected this. The model is, by its own accounting, never wrong.

She doesn't cry at her desk. She has learned, this spring, a specific discipline about where and when to let things land.

She goes to Sector 9 straight from work, and finds Elias at the bench, not working, which by now she reads correctly as its own kind of message before he says anything at all.

"The Whitlocks were denied," she says, before he can speak first.

He's quiet for a moment. "I expected that outcome would come for someone before it came for Daniel. I didn't expect it this soon."

"What do we tell them?"

"That there's no more process left to offer them. That I'm sorry, which changes nothing, but which I'll say anyway, because saying nothing would be worse." He looks tired in a way that isn't only about the appeals. "I'll go with you, if you're the one delivering it. It shouldn't be a letter alone."

She looks at him for a moment, noticing something in his stillness she can't quite place — not just grief for the Whitlocks, something underneath it, held very carefully.

"Are you alright?" she asks.

"I will be," he says, which isn't an answer, and they both seem to know it, and neither of them pushes past the space where he's decided to stop.

They walk together to the Whitlocks' block as the evening comes down, saying little, the letter folded between them like something too heavy to carry in just one pair of hands.
