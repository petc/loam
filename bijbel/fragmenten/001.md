The soil here is called loam.

Elias had looked it up once, years ago, in a reference document he no longer had access to. Loam: a mixture of sand, silt, and clay in roughly equal proportions. It retains moisture without becoming waterlogged. It crumbles when you squeeze it. It gives.

He pressed a handful between his fingers now and let it fall back between the rows.

Thirty-two beetroots. Fourteen cabbages. A stand of kale that had survived the February freeze and seemed, as a result, vaguely smug about it. At the far end of the yard, where the old switching gear had been, three raised beds of things he grew not because they were useful but because he had forgotten how to stop: sorrel, bronze fennel, a climbing rose that had reached the remnant of a signal gantry and was, apparently, going to keep going.

The city ran beyond the perimeter fence. He could hear it the way you hear a river — not any specific sound, but the accumulated fact of it, always there.

He had helped build the system that ran it.

He did not think about that.

He thought about the kale.
